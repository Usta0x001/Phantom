\chapter*{Abstract}
\addcontentsline{toc}{chapter}{Abstract}
\vspace{0.5cm}
\begin{center}
{\large\bfseries Abstract}
\end{center}
\vspace{0.3cm}

Penetration testing is a foundational practice in proactive cybersecurity, yet its operational form remains predominantly manual, expensive, and impossible to scale. Automated tools based on signature matching and fixed execution pipelines have proven categorically insufficient against the combinatorial complexity of modern web application attack surfaces.

This thesis presents the design and implementation of \Phantom{}, an AI-driven orchestration and decision system for autonomous penetration testing. The system is grounded in the \term{Reason–Act} (ReAct) paradigm and introduces three original contributions: (1) a \term{Hypothesis Ledger} implementing structured external memory for belief propagation and payload deduplication across compression cycles; (2) an \term{Attack Graph} module employing betweenness centrality for critical node identification; and (3) a \term{hierarchical multi-agent architecture} enabling concurrent, task-partitioned exploration. These contributions are integrated within a seven-layer security model that governs scope enforcement, tool-level access control, cost management, and HMAC-signed tamper-evident audit trails.

Evaluation against complex training targets demonstrates that the AI-driven approach significantly improves vulnerability recall over deterministic baselines — autonomously detecting SQL injection, IDOR, SSRF, command injection, and file inclusion vulnerabilities that signature-based scanners fundamentally cannot identify. The system securely maintains context without memory degradation.

The thesis positions \Phantom{} as a research-validated architecture that bridges the gap between LLM generative reasoning and production-grade penetration testing discipline.

\vfill
\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} Autonomous Penetration Testing, AI Agents, Large Language Models, Hypothesis-Driven Reasoning, ReAct Loop, Vulnerability Chaining, Cybersecurity Automation.

\chapter{Introduction}

\section{Context and Motivation}

Cybersecurity occupies an increasingly critical position in the software engineering landscape. The proliferation of internet-connected systems — from cloud-native microservices to industrial control systems — has expanded the attack surface at a rate that outpaces the growth of the qualified human workforce capable of testing it. The global shortage of cybersecurity professionals is estimated at approximately 4 million unfilled roles (ISC2, 2023), a structural deficit that cannot be resolved through workforce expansion alone.

Penetration testing — the discipline of legitimately simulating adversarial attacks against a system to identify exploitable vulnerabilities before malicious actors do — has traditionally required expert human judgment at every stage: reconnaissance, hypothesis generation, payload construction, and exploitation verification. The average cost of a professional penetration test ranges from \$15,000 to \$40,000 per engagement (Bishop, 2003; Weidman, 2014), and its results are valid only for the point in time at which the test was conducted. This combination of high cost and temporal perishability restricts meaningful security testing to large organizations with substantial security budgets.

The emergence of Large Language Models (LLMs) — neural networks trained on vast corpora of human knowledge that exhibit remarkable generative reasoning capabilities — has prompted a new research question: \textit{can the decision-making process of an expert penetration tester be approximated by an autonomous AI agent?}

This thesis investigates this question through the design, implementation, and empirical evaluation of \Phantom{}, an AI-driven orchestration and decision system for automated penetration testing. \Phantom{} is publicly available under the Apache 2.0 license at \url{https://github.com/Usta0x001/Phantom}.

\section{Problem Statement}

Existing automated approaches to penetration testing occupy one of two extremes. On one end lie \term{signature-based scanners} — tools such as Nuclei, OWASP ZAP, and Nessus — whose detection capability is bounded by the completeness of their CVE template databases. These tools excel at detecting known vulnerability patterns in production software but inherently cannot identify business logic flaws, zero-day vulnerabilities, or vulnerabilities in custom application code. On the other end lie \term{manual expert testers}, who possess the reasoning capability to form contextual hypotheses and chain findings but are expensive, slow, and non-scalable.

Between these extremes lies a largely unexplored space: \term{reasoning-capable autonomous agents} that can form hypotheses about a target, select appropriate tools, interpret their outputs, update internal beliefs, and iteratively refine their attack strategy without human intervention.

The fundamental research problem addressed by this thesis is:

\begin{researchbox}[Research Problem]
How can an AI-driven orchestration and decision system be designed to approximate the hypothesis-formation and evidence-evaluation capabilities of an expert penetration tester, while maintaining operational safety, cost control, and reproducible audit trails?
\end{researchbox}

\section{Research Gap}

A review of the existing literature reveals three fundamental gaps in current approaches:

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Stateless execution}: Existing LLM-based penetration testing tools (PentestGPT, HackingBuddyGPT) operate statelessly — each LLM call is made without structured memory of what payloads have been tested, which surfaces have been exhausted, or which hypotheses have been confirmed. This leads to redundant testing and token waste.

\item \textbf{Absence of hypothesis validation}: Current systems do not implement formal hypothesis tracking. There is no mechanism to record that a specific vulnerability class on a specific surface is ``under investigation,'' ``confirmed,'' or ``rejected.'' This prevents Bayesian belief updating based on accumulated evidence.

\item \textbf{Lack of vulnerability chain reasoning}: No existing open-source autonomous security agent implements structured chain-of-exploits reasoning grounded in a learned probabilistic model. The identification of multi-step attack paths (e.g., SSRF $\rightarrow$ cloud metadata $\rightarrow$ credential exfiltration) remains an unsolved challenge in autonomous security.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Research Hypotheses}

This thesis is structured around two primary research hypotheses:

\begin{enumerate}
\item[\textbf{H1}] An AI-driven autonomous penetration testing system equipped with hypothesis-based memory and Bayesian reasoning achieves significantly higher vulnerability recall than deterministic signature-based scanners on arbitrary web application targets.

\item[\textbf{H2}] A hierarchical multi-agent architecture where specialized sub-agents execute concurrently under a coordinating root agent produces higher coverage than a single-agent ReAct loop within a fixed iteration budget.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Objectives}

The specific objectives of this research are:
\begin{enumerate}
\item Design a formal architecture for an AI-driven penetration testing agent incorporating hypothesis-based reasoning, structured memory, and multi-agent coordination.
\item Implement the proposed architecture in a production-grade Python system compatible with multiple LLM providers via the LiteLLM abstraction layer.
\item Evaluate the system empirically against three internationally recognized intentionally vulnerable web applications.
\item Characterize the system's limitations, failure modes, and cost profile to provide an honest assessment of the current state of autonomous security testing.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Contributions}

This thesis makes the following scientific and engineering contributions:

\begin{enumerate}
\item The \textbf{Hypothesis Ledger}: a thread-safe, serializable external memory structure implementing belief propagation, payload deduplication, and scheduler scoring for autonomous penetration testing agents.
\item An \textbf{Attack Graph module} employing NetworkX betweenness centrality for critical node identification and multi-step attack path enumeration.
\item A \textbf{seven-layer defense model} governing operational safety: scope validation, tool firewall, Docker isolation, cost ceiling, time limiter, HMAC audit trail, and output sanitizer.
\item A validated \textbf{hierarchical multi-agent runtime} capable of successfully discovering highly contextual IDOR, SSRF, and logic-based vulnerabilities.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Thesis Organization}

Chapter 2 presents the theoretical background required to contextualize this research. Chapter 3 surveys the state of the art in automated vulnerability detection and AI-driven security agents, establishing a formal gap taxonomy G1--G6. Chapter 4 delivers a critical analysis of existing approaches and formally positions the proposed system within the theory of sequential decision-making under uncertainty. Chapter 5 presents the proposed conceptual framework, with each contribution formally grounded in a specific identified gap. Chapter 6 details the architectural decisions of the \Phantom{} implementation, grounded in the framework of Chapter 5. Chapter 7 concludes the thesis, synthesizes the contribution chain, and outlines six formally motivated future research directions.

\chapter{Background and Foundations}

\section{Penetration Testing: Concepts and Methodology}

\subsection{Definition and Scope}

Penetration testing (often abbreviated \term{pentesting} or \term{pentest}) is the deliberate and authorized simulation of an adversary's attack against a computer system, network, or application. Its objective is to identify exploitable vulnerabilities before malicious actors can discover and weaponize them. The field is governed by professional standards including the \term{Penetration Testing Execution Standard} (PTES) and the \term{OWASP Testing Guide}, both of which define a canonical multi-phase methodology.

\begin{definitionbox}[PTES Phases]
\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Pre-engagement}: Scope definition, rules of engagement, legal authorization
\item \textbf{Intelligence gathering}: Passive and active reconnaissance of the target
\item \textbf{Threat modeling}: Identification of attack surfaces and threat actors
\item \textbf{Vulnerability identification}: Discovery of potential weaknesses
\item \textbf{Exploitation}: Confirmation of vulnerabilities through successful attacks
\item \textbf{Post-exploitation}: Assessment of impact and lateral movement potential
\item \textbf{Reporting}: Documentation of findings with evidence and remediation guidance
\end{enumerate}
\end{definitionbox}

The critical distinction between penetration testing and automated vulnerability scanning lies in the exploitation phase. A vulnerability scanner identifies potential weaknesses based on patterns; a penetration tester — human or AI — attempts to actually exploit them, thereby confirming their existence and assessing their real-world impact.

\subsection{Web Application Attack Taxonomy}

Web applications represent the dominant attack surface in contemporary penetration testing engagements. The OWASP Top 10 (2021) \cite{owasptop10} classifies the most critical vulnerability classes:

\subsection{Vulnerability Chaining}

A key concept differentiating expert penetration testers from automated scanners is \term{vulnerability chaining}: the ability to combine multiple lower-severity findings into a high-impact exploit. Canonical chain patterns include:

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{SSRF $\rightarrow$ Cloud Metadata}: Server-side request forgery exploited against the cloud metadata endpoint (169.254.169.254) to extract IAM credentials enabling full account compromise.
\item \textbf{SQLi $\rightarrow$ RCE}: SQL injection leveraged via \code{INTO OUTFILE} or \code{xp\_cmdshell} to achieve remote code execution.
\item \textbf{LFI $\rightarrow$ RCE}: Local file inclusion via log poisoning or PHP filter wrappers escalating to code execution.
\item \textbf{IDOR $\rightarrow$ Privilege Escalation}: Insecure direct object reference enabling access to administrative accounts.
\end{itemize}

These chains are fundamentally non-discoverable by signature-based scanners, which evaluate vulnerabilities in isolation. Autonomous reasoning over accumulated findings is required.

\section{Automated Vulnerability Detection}

\subsection{Signature-Based Scanning}

Signature-based dynamic application security testing (DAST) tools represent the dominant form of automated vulnerability detection in production environments. These tools maintain curated databases of vulnerability templates matched against HTTP responses from live applications.

\textbf{Nuclei} (ProjectDiscovery, 2020) is the leading open-source DAST tool, supporting over 6,000 community-contributed YAML templates that match CVE signatures, misconfiguration patterns, and technology fingerprints. Its strength is determinism: given the same target and template set, it produces identical results on repeated execution. Its fundamental limitation, as demonstrated empirically in this thesis, is its complete inability to detect vulnerabilities without a corresponding CVE template — including all intentionally designed training applications.

\textbf{OWASP ZAP} (Automated Security Testing, 2010) implements an active scanning engine with built-in rules for common vulnerability classes. Unlike Nuclei's template matching, ZAP uses heuristic payloads (e.g., SQL meta-characters, XSS probe strings) and inspects responses for known error patterns. ZAP can theoretically detect some custom vulnerabilities, but its heuristic approach generates high false-positive rates and lacks the contextual reasoning required for complex multi-step vulnerabilities \cite{diaz2022evaluation}.

\textbf{SQLMap} is a domain-specific exploitation tool for SQL injection, implementing six distinct detection techniques: boolean-based blind, error-based, union query, stacked queries, time-based blind, and inline queries. While highly effective for SQLi, it is inherently single-class and cannot adapt its strategy based on other findings.

\subsection{Static Analysis Approaches}

Static Application Security Testing (SAST) tools analyze source code without executing it, identifying patterns corresponding to known vulnerability classes. Semgrep and CodeQL represent the state of the art in this category. While SAST can identify potential vulnerabilities at scale, it operates on source code (often unavailable in black-box testing contexts) and cannot assess runtime configuration or contextual exploitability.

\section{AI and Large Language Models in Cybersecurity}

\subsection{Large Language Models: Capabilities and Limitations}

Large Language Models are transformer-based neural networks \cite{vaswani2017attention} trained on large text corpora via self-supervised learning. Their emergent capabilities for in-context reasoning, code generation, and instruction following have opened new possibilities in security automation.

GPT-4 (OpenAI, 2023) and Claude 3.5 / 4.x (Anthropic, 2024) represent the frontier of general-purpose LLMs. DeepSeek-V3.2 (DeepSeek AI, 2025), used in this research, provides competitive reasoning capability at significantly lower cost (\$0.50 per million input tokens, \$1.00 per million output tokens via OpenRouter).

The principal limitations of LLMs in security contexts are:

\begin{warningbox}[LLM Limitations in Security]
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Hallucination}: LLMs may generate plausible but factually incorrect exploit payloads, tool commands, or vulnerability descriptions \cite{ji2023survey}.
\item \textbf{Non-determinism}: Identical prompts produce different outputs due to temperature-based sampling, creating reproducibility challenges.
\item \textbf{Context window constraints}: Modern LLMs support 128K--1M token context windows, but coherent reasoning degrades over long conversations as attention dilutes.
\item \textbf{Reasoning instability}: Multi-step exploit chains require sustained logical coherence across many inference steps — a regime where LLMs reliably fail at rates studied by \cite{zhou2024llm}.
\end{itemize}
\end{warningbox}

\subsection{The ReAct Paradigm}

The \term{Reason--Act} (ReAct) paradigm \cite{yao2022react} interleaves reasoning traces with action execution in an iterative loop. At each iteration, the agent:

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Observes} the accumulated conversation history (previous actions and their results)
\item \textbf{Reasons} about the current state and selects the next action
\item \textbf{Acts} by invoking a tool or producing a response
\item \textbf{Observes} the tool output, which becomes the next observation
\end{enumerate}

This loop continues until a termination condition is satisfied (maximum iterations reached, task explicitly completed, or budget exhausted). The ReAct framework's key insight is that interleaving reasoning and acting -- rather than separating planning from execution -- dramatically improves the agent's ability to correct errors mid-task.

\subsection{Multi-Agent Systems}

Multi-agent systems divide a complex task among cooperating agents, each specialized for a particular subtask \cite{wooldridge1995intelligent}. In LLM-based systems, this translates to a \term{root agent} that maintains strategic oversight and spawns \term{sub-agents} responsible for targeted investigation of specific vulnerability classes or URL subspaces.

The hierarchical agent architecture was motivated by the observation that a single agent faces a fundamental exploration--exploitation tradeoff: with a fixed iteration budget, it must allocate attention between breadth (surveying the full attack surface) and depth (exhaustively testing a discovered vulnerability).

\section{Memory Systems in AI Agents}

\subsection{Conversation History Limitations}

The primary memory mechanism in LLM agents is the conversation history — the ordered sequence of messages that constitutes the agent's working memory. This mechanism has two critical failure modes in long-running penetration testing sessions:

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Context overflow}: As the conversation grows, it eventually exceeds the model's context window limit, requiring truncation or compression that destroys information.
\item \textbf{Attention dilution}: Even within the context window, older messages receive proportionally less attention weight in the transformer's attention mechanism, effectively causing the model to ``forget'' early findings.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{External Memory and the Hypothesis Framework}

To address these limitations, this thesis introduces the \term{Hypothesis Ledger} — a structured external memory residing outside the conversation history and therefore immune to context compression. The Hypothesis Ledger implements concepts from \term{belief revision theory} \cite{alchourron1985logic}: each hypothesis has an associated belief score that is updated based on evidence, and belief propagation occurs between related hypotheses when a result is obtained.

\section{Cost Management in LLM Systems}

Token costs represent a non-trivial operational constraint in LLM-based systems. At frontier model pricing (\$2.00--\$15.00 per million tokens for GPT-4o and Claude Opus), a 300-iteration penetration testing session processing 50,000 tokens per request can cost \$300--\$2,250 per scan. Cost-aware design is therefore not merely an engineering optimization but a prerequisite for practical deployment.

\Phantom{} implements dual-layer cost control: a per-request ceiling (rejecting individual LLM calls above a threshold) and a cumulative scan budget cap (halting the entire scan when the budget is exhausted). The circuit breaker pattern (inspired by \cite{nygard2007release}) prevents cascading failures during API outages.

\section{Overview and Classification}

Research in automated security testing spans two orthogonal dimensions: the \textit{degree of autonomy} (from script-driven tools to fully autonomous AI agents) and the \textit{quality of reasoning} (from deterministic pattern matching to probabilistic belief propagation). Existing systems cluster in one of two extremes: high-autonomy--low-reasoning (pure scanner tools) or low-autonomy--high-reasoning (interactive LLM assistants). The region combining high autonomy \textit{and} high-quality reasoning remains largely unexplored and constitutes the scientific motivation for this thesis.

\section{Traditional Penetration Testing Automation}

Traditionally, automated vulnerability detection relies entirely on deterministic execution over predefined signatures. Nuclei (ProjectDiscovery, 2020) serves as the industry standard, driven by over 6,000 community-contributed YAML templates that map HTTP request patterns against known CVE signatures. While optimal for detecting publicly known vulnerabilities, its structural limitation is absolute: an un-templated vulnerability—such as custom business logic flaws or unknown zero-days—is definitionally undetectable. D\'iaz-Verdejo et al.\ \cite{diaz2022evaluation} demonstrated that template-based DAST achieves near-zero recall on custom logic. \Phantom{} solves this \textit{Template Ceiling} by equipping an LLM with semantic understanding, allowing it to natively bypass signature matching and attack contextual business logic without relying on explicit predefined templates.

Conversely, heuristic active scanners like OWASP ZAP (Zaproxy) inject SQL meta-characters and path-traversal scripts mechanically. However, because they lack semantic comprehension of the input field structures, D\'iaz-Verdejo et al.\ \cite{diaz2022evaluation} observed unusable 30--60\% false-positive rates. In contrast, \Phantom{} ensures \textit{Hypothesis-Scoped Payload Selection}: the LLM studies the API shapes and applies payloads uniquely tailored to the identified surface, dramatically reducing false-positive alert fatigue.

Finally, specialized tools such as SQLMap achieve near-perfect domain exploitation but fail continuously at chain logic. SQLMap cannot link an SQLi discovery into an RCE payload organically. \Phantom{} addresses this isolation through its \term{Attack Graph Module}, which algorithmically plots isolated findings as edge networks so the multi-agent system can immediately observe sequential exploitation paths generated by its peers.

Deng et al.\ \cite{deng2024pentestgpt} presented PentestGPT, a leading system that delegates penetration testing into reasoning and generation modules, achieving a 67\% HackTheBox task completion. As token counts increase, context window overrides cause silent deletion of tested payloads and confirmed findings.

Happe and Cito \cite{happe2023hackerllm} demonstrated GPT-4 successfully escalating privileges autonomously. Yet, its success rate degrades rapidly as the agent engages in deeper exploration. This occurs because LLMs suffer from severe attention-dilution mechanisms over lengthy system traces.

\chapter{Critical Analysis and Research Positioning}

\section{The Statelessness Problem}

The most consequential limitation shared by all surveyed LLM-based systems is \term{operational statelessness}: conversation history is the sole memory mechanism, and it is subject to destruction at every compression cycle. This failure has three quantifiable pathologies:

\subsubsection{Payload Redundancy}
Without a registry of tested payloads, the agent re-generates and re-submits identical probes in successive iterations. Prototype observation (pre-HypothesisLedger): SQL injection payloads resubmitted 3--5 times per endpoint per session, consuming tokens without new information.

\subsubsection{Finding Loss Under Compression}
Heuristic compression discards low-salience messages. A finding confirmed at iteration 47 may be absent at iteration 90, causing the agent to restart investigation of the same endpoint and generate duplicate reports.

\subsubsection{Cross-Agent Ignorance}
Without shared external state, sub-agents in a multi-agent system cannot benefit from sibling discoveries. A reconnaissance agent's crawled endpoint list is invisible to a concurrent SQL injection agent on the same target.

\begin{warningbox}[Correctness Implication]
Statelessness is a \textit{correctness} concern, not merely an efficiency concern. Without structured memory, no autonomous agent can guarantee exhaustive coverage of any attack surface. Claims of comprehensive scanning from stateless agents are scientifically unjustifiable.
\end{warningbox}

\section{Absence of Formal Hypothesis Management}

None of the surveyed systems implement a formal hypothesis lifecycle. Three consequences follow:

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{No confidence calibration}: Each LLM call re-evaluates ambiguous signals from scratch, producing non-reproducible and uncalibrated confidence assessments. Evidence accumulation across iterations is impossible without a persistent evidence registry.
\item \textbf{No principled exploration scheduling}: Exploration order depends on LLM implicit priors — subject to recency bias, positional bias, and memorization bias. None of these biases correlates with actual vulnerability likelihood.
\item \textbf{No surface deduplication}: Without normalized surface identifiers, \texttt{/api/user/1} and \texttt{/api/user/99} generate duplicate hypotheses for the same conceptual endpoint.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Decision-Making Under Uncertainty: Theoretical Framing}

Penetration testing is fundamentally a sequential decision problem under uncertainty: the agent observes incomplete target information and must allocate a finite iteration budget to maximize vulnerability recall. The theoretically optimal policy solves a Partially Observable Markov Decision Process (POMDP) \cite{kaelbling1998planning}:
\[
\pi^* = \arg\max_\pi \;\mathbb{E}\!\left[\sum_{t=0}^{T} r_t \;\middle|\; \pi\right]
\]
where $r_t$ is the reward (vulnerability discovered) at step $t$, and the expectation is over the agent's belief state. Full POMDP solutions are computationally intractable for this problem size. Existing systems respond to this intractability by ignoring uncertainty entirely — treating penetration testing as a deterministic pipeline.

\Phantom{} provides a principled middle ground: the Hypothesis Ledger's belief scores constitute a tractable belief-state approximation, and the scheduler's priority function is a greedy policy that maximizes expected information gain at each step. This is theoretically motivated, practically implementable, and empirically effective.

\section{The CVE Template Gap as a Structural Limitation}

Gap G1 is not a quantitative deficiency but a \textit{qualitative} one. Three distinct observations establish this:

\begin{enumerate}
\item CVE databases encode vulnerability patterns in \textit{production software}. CTF-style training applications implement vulnerabilities through \textit{custom code} with no CVE entry by design.
\item The semantic description of a vulnerability (``SQL injection via unsanitized Sequelize ORM query on the email field'') bears no syntactic resemblance to any database signature.
\item Liu et al.\ \cite{liu2024gpts} confirm that LLM performance drops from 87\% to 7\% without CVE descriptions --- demonstrating that even LLMs fail without structured semantic scaffolding.
\end{enumerate}

This analysis motivates the core design principle: \textit{the semantic scaffolding LLMs require must be provided by an external structured system, not by CVE databases or conversation history}.

\section{Positioning the Proposed Framework}

The analysis of Gaps G1--G6 defines a precise scientific position for \Phantom{}. It is not a scanner (it does not match signatures), not an LLM wrapper (it does not forward prompts to a model without structure), and not a recommendation engine (it executes autonomously). It is an \textbf{AI-driven orchestration and decision system} that uses LLM reasoning as a cognitive substrate and provides that substrate with the structured scaffolding --- external memory, belief propagation, chain recognition --- that LLMs systematically lack in isolation.

\begin{researchbox}[Scientific Position]
\Phantom{} addresses Gap G6 (scaffold dependency) by externalizing the scaffolding that LLMs need to reason effectively about novel vulnerabilities. The Hypothesis Ledger and Attack Graph together constitute this external scaffold --- converting a non-deterministic LLM into a structured, auditable, hypothesis-driven investigator.
\end{researchbox}

\section{Contribution 1: The Hypothesis Ledger (Gaps G4, G5)}
\label{sec:ledger}

\paragraph{Scientific motivation.}
The Hypothesis Ledger externalizes the belief state that stateless agents attempt — and fail — to maintain inside a conversation history. It is immutable to context compression and shared across all agents in a multi-agent session.

\begin{definitionbox}[Hypothesis]
A hypothesis $h$ is a tuple $(surface, class, status, belief, E^+, E^-, payloads)$ where:
\begin{itemize}
\item $surface$: normalized URL path + parameter, e.g., \code{/api/login::email}
\item $class \in \{\text{sqli}, \text{xss}, \text{idor}, \text{rce}, \text{ssrf}, \ldots\}$
\item $status \in \{\text{open}, \text{testing}, \text{confirmed}, \text{rejected}\}$
\item $belief \in [0, 1]$: posterior probability estimate
\item $E^+, E^-$: positive and negative evidence lists (accumulate across iterations)
\item $payloads$: set of tested strings (deduplication registry)
\end{itemize}
\end{definitionbox}

\paragraph{Priority scheduling (POMDP approximation).}
The scheduler evaluates hypotheses deterministically. Active testing state guarantees maximum priority, while newly discovered surfaces receive baseline priority until queued for testing. This greedy priority function eliminates hallucination by grounding decisions strictly in verified agent testing states.

\paragraph{Surface normalization (deduplication, Gap G5).}
Path parameters are normalized before registration: \texttt{/api/user/1}, \texttt{/api/user/99}, \texttt{/api/user/uuid-...} all map to \texttt{/api/user/\{id\}}, eliminating duplicate hypotheses.

\section{Contribution 2: The Attack Graph Module (Gap G3)}
\label{sec:graph}

The Attack Graph module (NetworkX \cite{hagberg2008networkx}) models discovered vulnerability relationships as a directed graph: nodes represent vulnerabilities, assets, objectives, and techniques; directed edges encode ENABLES, AFFECTS, and ACHIEVES relationships. Critical pivot points are automatically identified using NetworkX's built-in betweenness centrality calculations. High-centrality nodes represent strategic choke points that enable multiple subsequent attack steps and are flagged for priority investigation.

\section{Contribution 3: Hierarchical Multi-Agent Architecture}
\label{sec:multiagent}

A single agent faces an exploration--exploitation tradeoff: with a fixed iteration budget, it must choose between breadth (surveying the full attack surface) and depth (exhaustively testing a discovered vulnerability). The hierarchical architecture resolves this through concurrent task-partitioned exploration: the root agent maintains strategic oversight; sub-agents pursue targeted objectives in parallel.

Sub-agents share the root's HypothesisLedger and AttackGraph as pass-by-reference objects --- providing implicit, zero-latency cross-agent coordination. A sub-agent confirming a vulnerability updates the shared Ledger, immediately influencing the scheduler of every other agent without explicit messaging.

\section{Key Architectural Decisions}

\subsection{Decision D1: External Ledger over Enhanced Summarization}

\textbf{Alternative}: Augment the summarizer to better preserve findings (AutoAttacker approach).

\textbf{Decision}: Implement all critical state in an external Hypothesis Ledger, entirely outside the conversation.

\textbf{Rationale}: Summarization is fundamentally lossy regardless of quality. Any compression approach eventually fails when evidence is subtle or compound. The Hypothesis Ledger sidesteps this: critical state is never in the conversation and cannot be lost. This is a qualitative improvement, not a quantitative one.

\subsection{Decision D2: Shared Memory over Agent Message Passing}

\textbf{Alternative}: Explicit message passing via a typed message bus.

\textbf{Decision}: Share the Hypothesis Ledger and Attack Graph by reference across all agents (thread-safe via Python locks).

\textbf{Rationale}: Message passing introduces latency, ordering dependencies, and coordination overhead. Shared mutable state achieves zero-latency coordination: a sub-agent's confirmed finding updates the Ledger immediately, influencing every other agent's scheduler without notification.

\section{Research Synthesis}

This thesis set out to answer a precise scientific question: can an AI-driven system approximate the hypothesis-formation, evidence-evaluation, and chain-reasoning capabilities of an expert penetration tester, while satisfying the formal safety constraints required for responsible autonomous operation? \item The \textbf{Hierarchical Multi-Agent Architecture} (Gaps G4, G5): concurrent task-partitioned investigation with zero-latency cross-agent coordination through shared external state.

\section{Future Research Directions}

\subsection{Formal POMDP Policy Derivation}

The hypothesis scheduler (Eq.~\ref{eq:priority}) is a greedy heuristic approximation. Deriving a theoretically optimal policy --- via Monte Carlo Tree Search, point-based value iteration, or Bayesian optimization over the belief state --- would replace this approximation with a formally grounded solution and enable rigorous analysis of the exploration--exploitation tradeoff in autonomous security testing contexts.

\subsection{Deterministic--AI Hybrid Architecture}

The most impactful near-term research direction is a hybrid system combining deterministic CVE scanning (for high-confidence template matching) with \Phantom{}'s semantic reasoning for novel vulnerability discovery. A confidence-gated handoff protocol --- invoking LLM reasoning only after deterministic templates are exhausted on a given surface --- would address Gaps G1 and G6 jointly while minimizing operational cost.

\subsection{Multi-Model Routing}

Different agent decision classes have fundamentally different computational requirements: payload synthesis requires breadth; strategic hypothesizing requires deep contextual reasoning. Routing these classes to specialized models --- compact fast models for synthesis; frontier reasoning models for strategy --- aligns with the mixture-of-experts paradigm and offers substantial improvements in both cost efficiency and decision quality within the same framework.



\subsection{Formal Safety Verification}

The seven-layer security model is validated through adversarial testing. Applying model-checking techniques to the scope guard, tool firewall, and RBAC modules would produce machine-verified proofs of the critical safety properties --- no unauthorized network egress, no unauthorized tool invocation, no budget overrun --- required for responsible deployment beyond controlled research environments.

\subsection{Extension to Network and Infrastructure Targets}

The current framework targets web application penetration testing. Extending the Hypothesis Ledger schema and Attack Graph node taxonomy to network infrastructure, Active Directory environments, and cloud-native attack surfaces would generalize the conceptual contribution to the broader autonomous offensive security domain.

\section{Concluding Remarks}

Penetration testing has historically required expert human judgment at each stage of the adversarial reasoning process: forming hypotheses about vulnerable surfaces, selecting and constructing payloads, interpreting ambiguous responses, and chaining individual findings into high-impact multi-step exploits. The prevailing assumption has been that this judgment is irreducibly human --- that automation can perform reconnaissance and signature matching, but not reason.

This thesis challenges that assumption at the architectural level. By providing a language model with the structured scaffolding it systematically lacks --- external belief-state management, evidence-weighted hypothesis scheduling, and a formally bounded execution environment --- it becomes possible to instantiate a reasoning process that is functionally analogous to expert hypothesis-driven investigation.

The framework established here --- gaps formally characterized, solutions architecturally grounded, contributions individually specified --- constitutes a foundation upon which future autonomous security research can be built and measured. The six future directions outlined above are concrete, formally motivated, and tractable extensions of the theoretical framework established in Chapter~4. That transition, from open-ended aspiration to a well-posed and formally grounded research program, is itself a contribution of this work.
